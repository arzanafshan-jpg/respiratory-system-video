Question 16 4 pts
Explain the role of the epiglottis and why it is important during swallowing. What happens if it fails to function correctly?
✦ Model Answer
The epiglottis is a leaf-shaped flap of cartilage located at the base of the tongue above the larynx. During swallowing, it reflexively folds downward to cover the opening of the larynx (glottis), preventing food or liquids from entering the trachea and lungs. This ensures that ingested material is directed into the esophagus (digestive tract) rather than the airway. If the epiglottis fails to close properly — due to neurological problems, muscle weakness, or disease — food or liquid can enter the airway (aspiration), leading to choking, aspiration pneumonia, or respiratory distress.

Question 17 4 pts
Describe the process of inhalation. What anatomical structures are involved and what pressure changes occur?
✦ Model Answer
During inhalation, the diaphragm (a dome-shaped muscle below the lungs) contracts and flattens, moving downward by 1–10 cm. Simultaneously, the intercostal muscles contract to expand the rib cage outward and upward. Together, these actions increase the volume of the thoracic (chest) cavity. By Boyle's Law, as volume increases, intra-pulmonary pressure drops by approximately −2 to −3 cmH₂O below atmospheric pressure. This pressure gradient causes air to rush in through the nose or mouth, down through the pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and finally into the alveoli. The entire process lasts about 1.5–2 seconds for a normal tidal breath (~500 mL).

Question 18 4 pts
What are alveoli? Describe their structure and explain how their design maximizes efficiency of gas exchange.
✦ Model Answer
Alveoli are tiny, balloon-like air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange occurs. There are approximately 300 million in the human lungs. Each alveolus is about 0.2 mm in diameter with an ultra-thin wall of only ~0.5 µm — thinner than a soap bubble. They are surrounded by a dense network of capillaries so narrow that red blood cells must pass through single-file. Their design maximizes gas exchange efficiency through: (1) enormous collective surface area (~70 m² — the size of a tennis court), (2) extremely short diffusion distance across the wall, and (3) high capillary density ensuring constant blood flow. O₂ diffuses from the alveolar air into the blood along a concentration gradient, while CO₂ moves in the opposite direction. The entire exchange occurs in under one millisecond.

Question 19 4 pts
Compare the upper and lower respiratory tracts. Name at least three structures from each and describe the overall pathway of air from the environment to the alveoli.
✦ Model Answer
The upper respiratory tract includes the nasal cavity, pharynx (throat), and larynx (voice box). Air first enters the nasal cavity (~10 cm long), where mucous membranes and cilia filter particles ≥10 µm, warm air to ~34°C, and humidify it. It then passes through the pharynx (~12–14 cm long, shared with the digestive system) and into the larynx (~4–5 cm, containing the vocal cords and epiglottis). The lower respiratory tract includes the trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and alveoli. From the larynx, air enters the trachea (~11 cm, reinforced by 16–20 cartilage rings), which bifurcates at the carina (T4–T5) into the right and left main bronchi. These branch 23 times (bronchioles becoming progressively smaller) until reaching the terminal and respiratory bronchioles, finally emptying into the alveoli where gas exchange occurs. The total airway pathway is approximately 2,400 km if stretched.

Question 20 4 pts
A patient has a damaged diaphragm following a traumatic injury. Predict the consequences for their breathing. How might the body compensate?
✦ Model Answer
The diaphragm performs approximately 80% of the work of normal breathing. Damage to it would severely impair inhalation: the patient would be unable to generate the normal downward movement of the diaphragm that increases thoracic volume and drops intrapulmonary pressure. Consequently, tidal volume (normally ~500 mL) would decrease significantly, leading to shallow breathing, reduced gas exchange, hypoxia (low O₂) and hypercapnia (elevated CO₂). The body might compensate by recruiting accessory muscles of respiration — including the intercostal muscles, scalenes, sternocleidomastoid, and abdominal muscles — to expand the rib cage and expel air. In severe cases, mechanical ventilation may be required. Residual volume (~1,200 mL) would remain in the lungs but functional reserve capacity would be reduced, increasing the risk of atelectasis (lung collapse).
